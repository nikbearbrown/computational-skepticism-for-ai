# Research Notes: Chapter 08 — Robustness: What "Understanding" Means When a Pixel Can Break the Model
**Corresponding chapter:** chapters/08-robustness-what-understanding-means-when-a-pixel-can-break-the-model.md · **Editor note:** notes/08-robustness-what-understanding-means-when-a-pixel-can-break-the-model.md · **Generated:** 2026-07-01

## Chapter summary
The chapter reframes adversarial examples as an epistemological instrument: a model that flips its prediction under an imperceptible perturbation is not "fragile" but "honest about what it learned" — it learned a *proxy* feature that correlated with the label in training but is separable from the human-relevant feature at deployment. It argues (via a scaling-law result) that the proxy/human-feature gap cannot be closed by the engineering toolkit alone, and escalates to a Pearl Rung-3 "governance counterfactual" the toolkit provably cannot reach.

- **Claim:** Adversarial examples exist because the small perturbation, aligned with the weight vector, accumulates linearly across high-dimensional inputs (ε‖w‖₁). · **Source:** Goodfellow et al. 2015, arXiv:1412.6572 · primary · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED — the "linear explanation of adversarial examples" section.
- **Claim:** Adversarial vulnerability originates in the *phenomenon itself* — discontinuous input-output mappings and "blind spots" in high-dimensional space. · **Source:** Szegedy, Zaremba, Sutskever, Bruna, Erhan, Goodfellow, Fergus, "Intriguing Properties of Neural Networks," 2014, ICLR; arXiv:1312.6199, https://arxiv.org/abs/1312.6199 · primary · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED — original adversarial-examples paper; supports the "not a bug we introduced" framing.
- **Claim:** Adversarial examples are not bugs but *non-robust features* — genuinely predictive patterns in the data that are imperceptible to humans; models can generalize from them alone. · **Source:** Ilyas, Santurkar, Tsipras, Engstrom, Tran, Madry, "Adversarial Examples Are Not Bugs, They Are Features," 2019, NeurIPS 32; arXiv:1905.02175, https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.02175 · primary · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED — this is the direct source for the chapter's "features not bugs" / proxy-feature spine.
- **Claim:** There is a provable tension between adversarial robustness and standard accuracy; robust models learn different feature representations. · **Source:** Tsipras, Santurkar, Engstrom, Turner, Madry, "Robustness May Be at Odds with Accuracy," 2019, ICLR; arXiv:1805.12152, https://arxiv.org/abs/1805.12152 · primary · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED — supports the "you cannot simply train it away for free" cost argument.
- **Claim:** Adversarial (robust) training via PGD is the reference defense; robustness comes at increased training cost. · **Source:** Madry, Makelov, Schmidt, Tsipras, Vladu, "Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks," 2018, ICLR; arXiv:1706.06083, https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.06083 · primary · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED for PGD + robust-training framing. The specific "≈9× more expensive than standard training" figure is NOT stated in this abstract — see D (Open flags).
- **Claim:** On CIFAR-10, state-of-the-art robustness to ℓ∞ perturbations barely exceeds 70%, and scaling laws predict robustness "slowly grows then plateaus at 90%," so surpassing it by scale is impractical; human performance ALSO plateaus near 90% because ℓ∞-constrained attacks generate invalid images inconsistent with their labels. · **Source:** Bartoldson, Diffenderfer, Parasyris, Kailkhura, "Adversarial Robustness Limits via Scaling-Law and Human-Alignment Studies," 2024, ICML; arXiv:2404.09349, https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.09349 · primary · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED for the 90% ceiling AND the semantic reason (perturbed images resemble/are invalid for the target class). The **$10^{30}$ FLOPs figure is NOT in the abstract** — see B and D.
- **Claim:** Any classifier that performs well under Gaussian noise can be turned into one certifiably robust in ℓ₂ via randomized smoothing (closed-form certified radius). · **Source:** Cohen, Rosenfeld, Kolter, "Certified Adversarial Robustness via Randomized Smoothing," 2019, ICML (PMLR v97, pp. 1310–1320), https://proceedings.mlr.press/v97/cohen19c.html · primary · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED — certified 49% top-1 on ImageNet at ℓ₂ radius 0.5; the certified-radius formula the chapter cites is from this paper.
- **Claim:** α,β-CROWN is a current, complete GPU-accelerated neural-network verifier that scales to real architectures. · **Source:** Verified-Intelligence, "alpha-beta-CROWN," https://github.com/Verified-Intelligence/alpha-beta-CROWN ; VNN-COMP 2025 summary, arXiv:2512.19007 · primary (tool + competition report) · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED — winner of VNN-COMP 2021–2025; supports general architectures incl. transformers, but complete verification remains bounded to modest networks/specs (scope caveat in D).
- **Claim:** A governance/standards framework treats robustness as one trustworthiness characteristic among several (socio-technical framing). · **Source:** NIST, "Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)," NIST AI 100-1, January 2023, https://www.nist.gov/publications/artificial-intelligence-risk-management-framework-ai-rmf-10 · primary · **Verdict:** CONFIRMED — Jan 2023, four functions GOVERN/MAP/MEASURE/MANAGE; robustness listed among trustworthiness characteristics.

## C. Domain examples / cases (real, cited)
- **Image (flagship):** panda + imperceptible noise → "gibbon" at 99.3% confidence — Goodfellow et al. 2015, Fig. 1 (arXiv:1412.6572). CONFIRMED.
- **CIFAR-10 robustness ceiling:** SOTA ℓ∞ robustness ≈70%, predicted plateau ≈90%; human accuracy on the fooling set also ≈90% because attacks produce label-invalid images — Bartoldson et al. 2024 (arXiv:2404.09349). CONFIRMED.
