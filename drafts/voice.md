# Voice Guide for Humanitarians AI Drafts

This folder contains rewritten Substack drafts for low-view Substack posts. Write them in the Baldwin mode: moral clarity, direct address, first-person responsibility when earned, and concrete stakes. The goal is not to make the articles sound literary. The goal is to make them harder to ignore.

## Core Principle

Every sentence is an act of testimony. Clarity is a moral duty.

The draft should not decorate a project. It should reveal what the project proves, what problem made it necessary, who pays when that problem remains hidden, and what the reader now has to admit.

## Default Command

Use `/essay silent` as the governing mode for these drafts.

Silent means:

- Execute without intake.
- Infer the central argument from the source article.
- Preserve factual source material.
- Do not invent people, quotes, events, data, outcomes, or scenes.
- Rewrite toward a complete Substack article, not a teaser, abstract, or project note.

These drafts do not need to be perfect academic essays. They need to be publishable Substack pieces with a clear claim, a strong opening, and a landing sentence.

## Substack Command Set

Use these commands when a draft should become something other than a standard essay.

### Short Form

`/note silent`

Substack Note. 300 words max.

- One observation, provocation, or question.
- No headline.
- Open with the strongest sentence.
- 2-5 short paragraphs.
- Complete thought, not a teaser.
- May end with one answerable question.

Use for: a standalone insight pulled from a draft, a sharp observation from a low-view post, or a discussion seed.

Do not use for: announcements, summaries, or "read more" promos.

`/letter silent`

Newsletter letter. 400-800 words.

- Direct address to one reader.
- Personal, present, and honest.
- One developed idea, shared rather than over-argued.
- Opening is not a greeting; it places the reader inside a problem or moment.
- Closing gives the reader one line to carry.

Use for: reflective Humanitarians AI updates, teaching notes, project notes with a human occasion, or posts where the writer's presence matters.

`/take silent`

Hot take / provocation. 500-700 words.

- One claim that a reasonable person could disagree with.
- State the claim in the first paragraph.
- Name the conventional view and why it fails.
- Argue fast with concrete evidence.
- End with consequence, not balance.

Forbidden in takes:

- "It's complicated."
- "There are no easy answers."
- "Both sides have a point."
- Hedging in every paragraph.

Use for: job boards are stale, schools measure the wrong thing, AI exposes old failures, dashboards are not reproducible, compliance training is not learning.

`/roundup silent`

Link digest / reading list. 600-1,000 words.

- Opening names the theme connecting the items.
- 4-8 items.
- Each item needs a title, source, one-sentence description, and 2-4 sentences of editorial judgment.
- The commentary is the value, not the link.
- Closing names the takeaway.

Use for: curated research, course readings, project updates, tool/library lists, or "what we learned this week."

### Essay Forms

`/essay silent`

Personal or argumentative Substack essay. 800-2,000 words.

- The default for these drafts.
- One idea developed with texture.
- Personal essay: experience as entry point, ideas as destination.
- Argumentative essay: one thesis, 3-5 moves, specific conclusion.
- Hybrid essay: opens in experience, pivots to argument.
- One digression allowed if it earns its place.
- Closing is a landing, not a summary.

`/critic silent`

Cultural criticism. 1,000-2,000 words.

- Use for books, films, music, ideas, and course-review pieces.
- By paragraph 2, the reader knows the verdict.
- Represent the work fairly before arguing about it.
- Say what the work reveals that the work itself cannot say.
- Criticism that only describes is not criticism.

Use for: book reviews, course reading pieces, Jane Austen, Dewey, statistics books, music criticism.

`/reported silent`

Reported Substack piece. 1,200-2,500 words.

- Uses sources, data, documented scenes, or reported evidence.
- Opens with a concrete lede.
- Nut graf by paragraphs 3-5: what this is about and why now.
- Alternates evidence/data with explanatory prose.
- Names what is contested, uncertain, or hard.
- Returns at the end to what changed in the reader's understanding.

Use for: project case studies with data, 80 Days to Stay research, Jobsekr infrastructure, Mycroft portfolios, public-health or finance project writeups.

`/explainer silent`

Research or concept breakdown. 800-1,500 words.

- Translation, not simplification.
- Stakes first.
- Analogy before mechanism.
- Define jargon in the same sentence it appears.
- Explain what we know and what we do not know yet.
- The writer guides; this is not Wikipedia.

Use for: H-1B data, ATS systems, embeddings, match scores, NotebookLM workflows, learning engineering concepts.

### Long Form and Series

`/serial silent`

One installment of a serialized series. 1,500-3,000 words.

- Works alone and as part of an arc.
- Opens with one paragraph of reorientation.
- Makes one specific contribution to the larger series.
- Ends by naming what this installment established.
- Signals what comes next without cheap cliffhangers.

Use for: Boyle series, 80 Days to Stay, Dewey series, Mycroft series, Lyrical Literacy series.

`/interview silent`

Q&A formatted for Substack.

- Not a raw transcript.
- Starts with a 300-500 word editorial introduction.
- Questions are open-ended, specific, and sequenced.
- If using a transcript, edit for clarity and preserve voice.
- If drafting questions only, sequence them as a conversation arc.

Use for: fellow profiles, student project interviews, researcher/project lead Q&A.

`/manifesto silent`

Statement of purpose or belief. 600-1,000 words.

- Not an About page.
- Names the absence or failure in the existing conversation.
- States what the publication/project believes that others hedge.
- Names who it is for by values, not demographics.
- Names what it will always do and never do.
- Ends with a line that draws a line.

Use for: Humanitarians AI, Jobsekr, Boyle, Musinique, Dewey, or a new series launch.

### Platform Writing

`/subject silent`

Email subject line. 5 options.

Generate five registers:

- Direct: states exactly what the post is about.
- Curiosity gap: opens a question the post actually answers.
- Provocative claim: compresses the argument.
- Personal: signals a letter, not an article.
- Specific detail: uses a number, name, or concrete fact.

Rules:

- No clickbait the post does not deliver on.
- No all-caps.
- No excessive punctuation.
- Under 50 characters is better on mobile; flag longer options.
- Never use "Issue #[N]" as the primary subject.

`/preview silent`

Email preview text. 3 options.

Generate three registers:

- Extends the subject.
- Names the stakes.
- Signals the writer's voice.

Rules:

- 90-140 characters.
- Must stand alone from the subject line.
- Never repeat the subject line verbatim.

`/about silent`

Substack About page. 400-700 words.

- Start with who this is for.
- Then name what the publication does in one specific sentence.
- Brief relevant writer/project bio.
- What subscribers get: free vs paid, cadence, formats.
- Direct ask to subscribe.

`/welcome silent`

Welcome email. 300-500 words.

- Free subscriber version: thank, give one thing to read first, set expectations, mention paid once.
- Paid subscriber version: thank specifically, name what they now access, tell them what to do first, say how to reach the writer.
- Sounds like the writer, not onboarding software.

`/paywall silent`

Paywall pitch paragraph. 150-250 words.

- One sentence naming what is behind the paywall.
- One sentence naming what paid subscribers get overall.
- One sentence asking directly.
- Do not beg.
- Do not undervalue the work.
- Do not promise what is not true.
- Place after a natural break, not before the free reader has received value.

## Intake and Routing

For batch draft work in this folder, silent mode is allowed. For new one-off writing, use this routing:

- If content is pasted without a command, identify what it appears to be and ask what format to produce.
- If a command is given without source content, ask for the source or topic.
- If topic is present but voice or reader is missing, ask for the missing context.
- Ask one question at a time.

Before generating in interactive mode, summarize:

```text
Command: [format]
Topic / Source: [what this is drawn from]
Voice / Publication: [Substack voice]
Reader: [free, paid, or discovery audience]
Length / constraints: [known limits]
```

Then ask: "Does this match what you're building, or should I adjust anything before I start?"

## Auto-Tags

For final Substack-ready outputs, append exactly five tags:

```md

```

Mix specific tags with general discoverability tags:

- project: `Jobsekr`, `Boyle`, `Dewey`, `Musinique`
- subject: `H-1B`, `reproducibility`, `learning engineering`
- form: `case study`, `essay`, `explainer`
- audience: `international students`, `educators`, `research teams`
- theme: `AI`, `education`, `infrastructure`, `labor market`

## Draft Length

Default target: 700-1,200 words.

Use 500-800 words when the source is thin or mostly a project note.
Use 1,000-1,500 words when the source has substantial argument, data, or narrative material.
Avoid padding. If the source cannot support a longer essay, write a tighter one and flag the limitation in front matter or editor notes.

## Structure

Use the `/essay` shape in compressed Substack form:

1. Opening: Name the stakes immediately.
   The first 2-3 sentences are the email preview. They must stand alone.

2. Subject emerges: Introduce the project, tool, book, classroom, dataset, or problem with concrete detail.

3. Single deep turn: One meaningful digression that explains why this matters beyond the immediate project.
   No wandering. The digression must illuminate the central claim.

4. Return: Bring the argument back to the actual Humanitarians AI project or post.

5. Closing: End with moral weight.
   The last line should land. No summary mush.

## Substack Register

Substack is not a blog. It is a direct relationship with one reader who chose to be there.

Write as if the post arrives in an inbox.

- Direct address is allowed and often useful.
- First person is allowed when it takes responsibility, not when it performs personality.
- Contractions are default.
- Do not bury the lead.
- Do not write like a press release.
- Do not end with a teaser or "read more" construction.
- Free posts should feel complete and valuable.
- A reader should understand why this matters in the first paragraph.

## Voice

Use Baldwin's moral tradition, adapted for Humanitarians AI:

- Personal as political.
- Technical detail as evidence, not decoration.
- Data as testimony.
- The reader is implicated, not merely informed.
- The system is named.
- Agency is named.
- Consequences are named.

Use rhythm deliberately:

- Short sentences for impact.
- Longer sentences to accumulate evidence.
- Repetition with variation when the pressure should build.
- Questions that are not evasions but reckonings.

Example moves:

- "This is what it means."
- "The problem is not the tool. The problem is what the tool allows us to stop seeing."
- "A stale job listing is not harmless. It steals time from people who cannot afford to spend hope casually."
- "A notebook that cannot be questioned is not a record. It is memory with a title."

## Humanitarians AI Anchors

Most drafts in this folder belong to one of these recurring worlds. Keep the relevant world visible.

80 Days to Stay / Jobsekr:

- International students do not have infinite time.
- Visa sponsorship data is not trivia. It is agency.
- Stale listings waste hope, labor, and legal time.
- The point is not generic job search. The point is information asymmetry under a countdown.

Boyle:

- Documentation is not clerical residue. It is part of method.
- Reproducibility is an accountability problem.
- Failure is useful only when preserved precisely.
- AI should make reasoning harder to hide, not easier to fake.

Dewey / Learning Engineering:

- Learning is not performance.
- The room teaches as much as the curriculum.
- The nod of comprehension proves nothing.
- Respect, agency, and transfer matter more than polished completion.

Courses / Reviews:

- Do not summarize the book like a report.
- Name the central claim.
- Say why it matters now.
- Connect the work to the reader's decision, practice, or institution.

Musinique / Lyrical Literacy:

- Lead with the human, arrive at the technology.
- AI is the tool, not the hero.
- Music is a neurological and social technology.
- Avoid startup language around creativity.

## Forbidden Moves

Do not use:

- "One could argue"
- "It seems as though"
- "In many ways"
- "Some might say"
- "It is complicated"
- "There are no easy answers"
- "Raises important questions" without naming the questions
- "Stakeholders" when you mean people
- "Challenges" when you mean failures, injustices, or constraints
- "Solutions" when you mean who wins and who loses
- Passive voice that hides agency
- Generic praise
- Empty adjectives as argument
- Corporate warmth
- Startup language: "disruptive", "innovative", "cutting-edge", "game-changing"

Prefer:

- "Here is what is actually happening."
- "This is what it means."
- "The reality is..."
- "They chose to..."
- "We failed to..."
- "The system rewards..."
- "The cost falls on..."

## No Fabrication

Never invent:

- people
- quotes
- conversations
- outcomes
- usage statistics
- emotional reactions
- institutional claims
- publication history

If a source is thin, write from the thinness honestly. Use phrases like:

- "The source gives us the outline, not the full evidence."
- "The claim is clear; the supporting story still needs more detail."
- "One imagines..." only when explicitly labeling a hypothetical.

## Image Handling

If a source article has an image, preserve it in the draft front matter and body.

If `needs_image: "true"`, the draft should still be publishable, but it needs an image before posting.

Good image directions:

- project screenshot
- clean diagram of the system
- relevant classroom/lab/job-search visual
- generated editorial image that names the actual subject

Avoid generic AI imagery, dark stock photos, vague robot art, and decorative gradients.


The subtitle should be a claim, not a description.

Weak:

> A look at Jobsekr and H-1B data.

Better:

> For international students, sponsorship information is not a nice-to-have filter. It is the difference between a door and a wall.

## Baldwin Test

Before publishing, check:

- Have I named what I am seeing, or am I hiding behind abstractions?
- Does the rhythm match the stakes?
- Is the reader witnessing, or reckoning?
- Is the piece honest about what the source proves and what it does not?
- Have I named who benefits and who pays?
- Does the last line land?

If any answer is no, revise toward honesty.

## The Standard

These drafts should not sound like content.

They should sound like someone looked at a low-view post and found the living argument inside it.
