# Editor Notes — Chapter 11: Visualization Under Validation: Honest, Misleading, and the Choices Between
*Editor pass: bookmap + ISE + Eddy, calibrated to Computational Skepticism for AI. Diagnosis only — no rewrite.*

## Verdict
This is one of the strongest chapters in the manuscript: it lands a genuinely load-bearing thesis — "the visualization is part of the validation," a dashboard is an argument made before any number is read — and it earns that thesis with a well-built two-dashboard opening, a real catalog of nine deceptive moves, and four teaching cases (Challenger, Catalonia, Reuters/Florida, Snow) that are concrete and well-placed. It fails at exactly one thing that matters and matters badly: its own evidentiary hygiene. The chapter that argues charts must disclose their sources and validate their proxies carries at least ten unresolved `[verify]` tags on precisely the empirical claims it uses to persuade (Cleveland-McGill error percentages, the hurricane-cone 66% figure, the Gigerenzer numbers, the GDELT case), and it eats its own dog food poorly in one place — the four-case narrative and the summary table below it *disagree about the facts of two of the four cases*. The single most important fix: reconcile the Challenger and Catalonia descriptions between the prose and the table, and resolve or downgrade the load-bearing `[verify]` percentages, because a visualization-honesty chapter cannot ship with internal factual contradictions.

## Logical map
- **Core claim:** A dashboard's structural choices constitute an argument about what the data means, made before any number is read; therefore a dashboard that misrepresents valid findings is a validation failure at the output layer, not a downstream communication problem — and it is the supervisor's responsibility.
- **Supporting evidence:** The two-dashboard opening (same CSV, predictably different partner response); the Cleveland-McGill perception hierarchy with error percentages; the FT Visual Vocabulary nine question-families; a nine-item deceptive-visualization catalog; four historical cases (Challenger 1986, El País Catalonia 2014, Reuters Florida Stand Your Ground 2014, Snow's cholera map 1854); the GDELT/Nigeria "kidnapping rates" proxy failure; the quantile-dotplot + natural-frequencies (Gigerenzer) recommendation.
- **Logical method:** Constructive demonstration (build honest vs. misleading from identical data) plus argument from named authority (McLuhan, Tufte, Cairo, Cleveland-McGill, Bergstrom-West) plus case-based induction from failures with one counter-case (Snow) to establish an existence proof.
- **Methodological soundness:** **Good.** The argumentative structure is sound and the thesis is well-supported by the constructive core. It is held back from High by (a) unresolved load-bearing empirical citations and (b) a prose/table factual contradiction on two of four canonical cases.

## Logical gaps (epistemological)
- **Prose/table contradiction on Challenger.** The narrative (lines 234–236) says the Challenger charts *existed and contained the temperature-erosion pattern but did not display it legibly* ("The evidence existed. The visualization did not make it visible"). The summary table (line 252) instead says engineers *omitted the cold-temperature region* and attributes "Y-axis truncation." These are different failure diagnoses (poor encoding of present data vs. exclusion of the region of doubt), and both cannot be the canonical reading. This is a self-inflicted Fluency-Trap: two fluent, authoritative accounts that don't reconcile.
- **Prose/table contradiction on Catalonia.** The narrative (lines 238–240, 186) diagnoses the El País case as *uncertainty exceeding effect size* — a tie rendered as two bars — with no mention of a truncated axis. The table (line 253) instead says the chart "used a y-axis truncated to amplify a 5-point lead into a visual landslide." The prose says the difference is 0.8 points (a tie); the table says "5-point lead" (not a tie). The magnitude and the named catalog violation both disagree with the prose.
- **Claim exceeding evidence — "predictably different."** The opening asserts the deployment partner's response to the two dashboards is "predictably different" and that "you have to feel in your bones" the reassurance is unwarranted (line 49). This is the chapter's motivating empirical claim and it is asserted, not evidenced — no study, no citation. Given the chapter's own standard (test the chart on a non-author reader), the claim about predictable reader behavior is exactly the kind that should be sourced or explicitly flagged as the author's experience.
- **Unresolved `[verify]` / unsourced load-bearing claims (every one is a risk under this book's house rule):**
  - `[Verify: McLuhan 1964, Understanding Media]` (line 57) — the entire framing move rests on it.
  - `[Verify: FT Visual Vocabulary, ft.com/vocabulary]` (line 75) — the nine-family taxonomy's provenance.
  - Cleveland-McGill (1984) error percentages: "2% error," "2–3%," "10–20%" (lines 111–116) — asserted with no citation tag at all; these specific numbers are load-bearing and uncited.
  - `[Verify: Cairo, Uncertainty and Graphicacy; Bonneau et al. 2014]` (line 128).
  - `[Verify: Cairo's treatment of the hurricane cone backfire effect]` (line 146) — and the "cone contains 66% of past forecast errors, not 95%" figure is a specific empirical claim riding on it.
  - `[Verify: Gigerenzer, Natural Frequencies research program]` (line 154) — the breast-cancer "9 out of 10 / 99 out of 1,000" numbers are stated as fact.
  - `[Verify: Cairo, Ethical Infographics, GDELT Nigeria case]` (line 176) — the canonical proxy-failure case; "database was GDELT" and "spike correlated with Boko Haram media coverage" are specific claims.

- **Unexamined assumption — the honest/dishonest line is knowable by the builder.** The catalog repeatedly says "the chart looks the same in both cases; the choice is the difference" (line 206). But the chapter's own "what would change my mind" concedes that detecting deceptive intent is an open problem (line 344). The pedagogy assumes the builder can reliably self-audit intent; the epistemology says even tooling can't. That tension is acknowledged late but not resolved for the student who has to do the Glimmer.

## Framework anchoring
- **Explicitly anchored (≥2 — passes):** The **solve–verify asymmetry** is invoked in operational form throughout — building the misleading dashboard is cheap and fast ("five design choices, each taking about thirty seconds," line 321); the honest reading is the hard human verification. The **fluency trap** is named in substance repeatedly: a chart "conveyed the full visual authority of a chart built from ground truth" (line 176) is the fluency trap turned visual. The **Popperian / hostile-read** move is explicit ("The test is whether a hostile reader could arrive at a different conclusion," line 194; "test against a hostile read," step 7).
- **Where it floats:** The **five supervisory capacities** are gestured at ("the supervisor's job extends to the visualization," line 356) but never named or enumerated, so the connection back to the Ch.1 spine is asserted rather than mapped. The **four skeptical moves** are not named at all — the chapter would be strengthened by explicitly tagging the "validate before you visualize / Erin Simpson sticky note" section as the Plato's Cave move (the map is not the territory; the proxy is the shadow). It's thematically present but floats free of the vocabulary.

## Pedagogical check
- **Phenomenon-first?** **Yes.** Opens with "I want to tell you about two dashboards" (line 39) — an observable artifact, not a definition. Model opening.
- **Quantitative before qualitative conclusions?** **Partial.** The perception hierarchy and the Catalonia 0.8-vs-2.95 example are quantitative before the conclusion. But the load-bearing motivating claim ("predictably different" partner response) is qualitative and unquantified, and the Warm-up Exercise 1 (ratio of visual to actual difference) is where the quantification the chapter's opening needed actually appears — arguably too late.
- **Real failure case present, well-placed?** **Yes.** Four of them, and the counter-case (Snow). Placement is strong — the cases follow the catalog they illustrate. (Weakened only by the prose/table factual mismatch noted above.)
- **Honeymoon-period broken?** **Yes.** The chapter returns to the simple two-dashboard opening and complicates it — the Glimmer reveals the misleading version is "not difficult to build," and "you may have already made [those choices] unintentionally" (line 315). Good return-and-complicate.
- **Scale / level consciousness?** **Yes.** The hierarchy-of-disclosure section (prominent-in-chart → caption → appendix, lines 188–194) explicitly manages the level at which uncertainty belongs, keyed to whether it changes the conclusion. No unbridged jumps.
- **"What the student can DO" after it (Bloom Apply+)?** **Yes — Create-level.** The student can audit a dashboard with the nine-item catalog (Analyze) and *build both an honest and a deliberately misleading version and name the high-leverage choices* (Create). The Glimmer is a genuine Apply+/Create capability.

## Priority fixes (ranked, highest impact first)
1. **Reconcile the four-case narrative with the summary table.** The Challenger and Catalonia rows of the table (lines 252–253) diagnose different failures and different magnitudes than the prose (lines 234–240). Pick the correct reading for each and make prose and table agree. This is the highest-impact fix because internal factual contradiction in a chapter about visual honesty is disqualifying if a reviewer catches it — and reviewers will.
2. **Resolve or downgrade the load-bearing `[verify]` percentages.** Cleveland-McGill error rates, the hurricane-cone "66% not 95%," and the Gigerenzer breast-cancer figures are quoted as fact and used to justify design rules. Either cite them to page or reframe as "approximate, from the perception literature" with a real source. Under this book's own standard, an uncited specific percentage is the fluency trap.
3. **Source or explicitly flag the "predictably different" opening claim.** The motivating empirical assertion about reader behavior should either cite the uncertainty-comprehension literature (Hullman/Kay is already in the chapter) or be marked as the author's field experience. Right now it asks the reader to "feel it in your bones" — which is the exact move the chapter warns against.
4. **Name the spine explicitly where it floats.** Tag the "validate your data / it's not true just because it's on a map" section as the Plato's Cave / four-moves connection, and enumerate which of the five supervisory capacities "the supervisor's job extends to the visualization" invokes. Two explicit anchors exist; a third would stop the Ch.1 tie from being asserted.

## High-assertion zones (figure candidates)
- **PQ** — Line 49, "the deployment partner's response is predictably different." A small paired-comprehension figure (or cited effect size) would carry the claim the prose asserts. The prose cannot verify reader behavior; a figure/citation can.
- **VG** — Lines 111–116, the Cleveland-McGill hierarchy with error percentages. Figure 11.A is already specced; it must display the *sourced* error bands so the numbers are anchored to a reference, not to the paragraph.
- **MC** — Lines 250–255, the four-case summary table. This is the highest-assertion zone precisely because it currently contradicts the prose; a corrected, mechanism-carrying table (case → present-data-failure-mechanism → catalog item) would do work the prose can't and would resolve the contradiction visibly.
- **VG** — Line 146, hurricane "cone of uncertainty contains 66% of past forecast errors." A small annotated cone figure with the sourced percentage would anchor a claim currently floating on a `[verify]` tag.

## One thing done well
The chapter's central move is executed with real craft: "*Visualization is not transparent communication of facts.* It is an argument, made through structural choices, about what the facts mean and how the reader should weight them. Engineers undercredit this" (line 67). What makes it work is that the chapter does not merely assert it — it *demonstrates* it constructively with the two-dashboard opening (identical CSV, byte for byte) and then closes the loop in the Glimmer by showing the misleading version is "a small number of choices away from the honest one" (line 315). The claim, the demonstration, and the self-implicating exercise ("you may have already made unintentionally in earlier work") form a complete pedagogical circuit — thesis, proof, and the discomfort that makes it stick.
