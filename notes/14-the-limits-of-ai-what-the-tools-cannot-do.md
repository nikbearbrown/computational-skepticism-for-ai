# Editor Notes — Chapter 14: The Limits of AI: What the Tools Cannot Do
*Editor pass: bookmap + ISE + Eddy, calibrated to Computational Skepticism for AI. Diagnosis only — no rewrite.*

## Verdict
As the book's final chapter this does its job with unusual force: it names three categorical limits (meaning, intentionality, data-world gap), ties each back to where the reader met its operational form earlier (Hume in Ch.2, access-boundary in Ch.5, distribution shift in Ch.8), reframes AI as the latest entry in an extended-mind lineage, and lands the book's most important single sentence — that the supervisor's authority to *refuse* deployment is the most important authority in the system. The Taleb Mediocristan/Extremistan and turkey-problem import is the best-integrated outside idea in the manuscript, giving the data-world gap a sharper operational shape than the abstract distribution-shift literature. Where it is weakest is a fault it half-confesses: two of its three limits (meaning, intentionality) are *philosophically contested*, the chapter admits this, and then relies on an "operational consequence binds regardless" move to carry claims the underlying philosophy can't settle — a legitimate maneuver, but one that is asserted more than demonstrated, and that would not survive a determined functionalist objection at full strength. The single most important fix: make the "operational consequence binds regardless of the philosophy" argument explicitly and rigorously *once*, early, so it doesn't have to be re-asserted (and taken on faith) at each of the three limits.

## Logical map
- **Core claim:** Three limits — meaning, intentionality, the data-world gap — bound what AI can do regardless of capability scaling; they are structural, not contingent; and because they are, the supervisor's skepticism becomes a *safety mechanism* (not just methodology) in high-stakes deployments, culminating in an engineering practice that must include the authority to refuse deployment.
- **Supporting evidence:** The opening clinical-decision-support case (94% accuracy, three patients harmed, "the engineers designed the wrong tests"); the three-limit table with operational consequences; Turing (1950) and Searle (1980) each parsed for what they do and don't settle; Taleb's Mediocristan/Extremistan and the turkey problem; the AI cognitive profile (great at retrieval/synthesis/generation-under-spec; weak at problem-formulation/plausibility-auditing/interpretive-judgment; absent for accountability); the three-tier delegation map; the extended-mind lineage; the Legg-Hutter definition callback; the third calibration baseline closing the arc.
- **Logical method:** Categorical/structural argument (limits that scaling cannot close) plus philosophical demarcation (what Turing and Searle each settle) plus an "operational consequence binds regardless of the metaphysics" bridge that lets the chapter use contested premises without resolving them; the cognitive-profile → delegation-map section is derivation of a practical instrument from the structural claims.
- **Methodological soundness:** **Good.** The data-world-gap limit is rigorously argued and uncontested (the chapter says so honestly). The Turing/Searle demarcation is careful and resists the usual overclaims in both directions. It is held below High by the reliance on the "binds regardless" move to carry the two contested limits, and by several unresolved `[verify]` tags on the philosophical sources.

## Logical gaps (epistemological)
- **The "binds regardless" move is asserted, not proven.** For both meaning (line 65) and intentionality (line 73), the chapter concedes the philosophy is unsettled, then claims "the operational consequence binds regardless" — because "the system's behavior is inconsistent with the user's expectation of meaning often enough that the supervisor has to perform meaning-attribution." But *how often is often enough*, and does the frequency-of-mismatch argument actually establish a *categorical* limit, or only a *high-error-rate* limit that better models could reduce? A functionalist could grant every operational recommendation while denying the limit is categorical — which would weaken "regardless of capability scaling." The chapter needs to show that the mismatch is structural, not merely frequent; right now it slides from "frequent enough to matter" to "categorical" without bridging.
- **Unexamined tension — Limit 3 is categorical, Limits 1 and 2 may not be.** The chapter itself flags this: the data-world gap "is not contested the way the first two are" (line 79) and is "the limit I am most certain of." That candor is good, but it means the chapter is presenting three limits as a unified "categorical" set when by its own account only one is uncontestedly categorical. Grouping them as equally structural is a small overclaim the "what would change my mind" section (line 286) again partly walks back.
- **Claim exceeding evidence — the opening case.** The clinical-decision-support system that "passed every test... three patients were harmed" (lines 37–43) is the chapter's motivating case and its closing refrain ("The system passed every test. The engineers designed the wrong tests. Three patients were harmed," line 308). It carries no citation and reads as a composite/illustrative scenario. If composite, it should be marked as such — the book's own standard (and the calibration file's treatment of the analogous Swedish-triage case as `[verify]`) demands that a load-bearing failure narrative be sourced or explicitly labeled hypothetical. As written it has the full authority of a real case without the provenance.
- **Unresolved `[verify]` / unsourced load-bearing claims:**
  - `[Verify: Bender et al. 2021 stochastic-parrots framing; Chalmers 2023 on consciousness and LLMs; Searle 1980, Minds, Brains, and Programs]` (line 65) — the sources for the meaning-limit's contestation.
  - `[Verify: Turing 1950, Computing Machinery and Intelligence]` (line 97) — the entire Turing demarcation.
  - Unsourced-but-asserted: Taleb's *The Black Swan* (2007) and the Mediocristan/Extremistan/turkey material (lines 85–89) carry no tag though they are load-bearing; the Legg-Hutter definition attribution (line 238); the "Hampton sense" of metacognitive monitoring (line 288); the three calibration-baseline score ranges "0.4–0.6 / 0.6–0.75 / 0.7–0.85" (lines 274–276) are stated as empirical expectations with no data source — a strong claim about what "most students" score, uncited.
  - The opening clinical case (lines 37–43) — no citation, provenance unmarked.
- **Counter-reading not addressed — the strong functionalist.** The chapter parses Searle carefully and even concedes "embedding structures, attention patterns, and multimodal grounding" might "constitute something more" (line 103). But it never engages the strongest opposing position — that a sufficiently grounded multimodal system *would* close the meaning gap operationally — beyond the Application exercise A6 (which raises it and asks the student to rebut it). The chapter hands the hardest counterargument to an exercise rather than answering it in the body, which leaves the "regardless of capability scaling" claim partly undefended in the prose itself.

## Framework anchoring
- **Explicitly anchored (≥2 — passes strongly):** This chapter is the book's spine made explicit. The **four skeptical moves** are named and closed — Humean induction is "Hume's induction problem in Chapter 2" (line 81), and the whole data-world-gap limit is the Humean move at book scale. The **five supervisory capacities** are enumerated in operational form (Specify/problem-formulation named at line 187; plausibility auditing at 189; interpretive judgment at 191). The **solve–verify asymmetry** is the backbone of the cognitive profile ("AI produces fluent text that systematically overreaches its evidence," line 181; generation is cheap, plausibility-auditing is the human part). The **fluency trap** is named directly: "the verb taxonomy exists as a fluency-trap detector because AI produces fluent text that systematically overreaches its evidence" (line 181). All four spine elements are present and explicit — the strongest anchoring in the four chapters reviewed.
- **Where it floats:** Almost nowhere — this is the anchoring exemplar for the set. The only drift: the **Popperian falsifiability** move is present operationally (the rapid-prototyping section's "identify what evidence would falsify the assumption," line 147) but not named as Popper's move there, though the chapter names Popper elsewhere. Minor.

## Pedagogical check
- **Phenomenon-first?** **Yes.** "I want to tell you about a system that passed every test the engineers gave it" (line 37) — a concrete, disturbing observable before any framework. The strangeness *is* the lesson, stated as such (line 43).
- **Quantitative before qualitative conclusions?** **Partial.** The 94%-accuracy-three-patients framing is quantitative-anchored, and the calibration-baseline ranges are numeric. But the chapter is dominated by philosophical/qualitative argument (as its subject requires); the quant is illustrative rather than load-bearing.
- **Real failure case present, well-placed?** **Yes, with a caveat.** The opening clinical case is well-placed and recurs as the closing refrain — structurally excellent. Caveat: it is uncited/possibly composite (see Logical gaps), which for the book's *final* motivating case is a provenance risk.
- **Honeymoon-period broken?** **Yes.** The chapter's whole move is to break the honeymoon of "AI is powerful, delegate to it" — it returns to the extended-mind tools (histogram, prediction-lock, verb taxonomy) and complicates the temptation to treat AI as "a general replacement rather than a specific extension" (line 236). The "cognitive extremophile" reframe (line 238) is the complication delivered.
- **Scale / level consciousness?** **Yes.** The three-tier delegation map (delegate freely / with verification / not at all, lines 220–224) manages the level at which delegation is safe, keyed to whether the output is "checkable by inspection." The Mediocristan/Extremistan distinction is itself a scale-consciousness instrument (which world does the deployment live in?).
- **"What the student can DO" after it (Bloom Apply+)?** **Yes — Evaluate/Create, capstone-level.** Student can classify a deployment by which limit bites hardest, design the five-component engineering practice, and *stake a defensible proceed/modify/defer/refuse position using the verb taxonomy* (the Glimmer). This is the book's highest Bloom target and it is explicitly "the operational form of the entire book" (line 262).

## Priority fixes (ranked, highest impact first)
1. **Make the "operational consequence binds regardless of the philosophy" argument once, rigorously, up front — and show it establishes a *categorical* not merely *frequent* limit.** Right now it is re-asserted at each contested limit and never fully defended against the functionalist who grants the operations but denies the categoricalness. This is the highest-impact fix because "regardless of capability scaling" is the chapter's (and the book's closing) load-bearing modifier, and it is the one a strong reviewer will attack.
2. **Cite or explicitly mark the opening clinical case as composite/illustrative.** As the book's final motivating failure and closing refrain, it should not carry real-case authority without provenance. A one-line footnote ("a composite drawn from documented clinical-AI failures") would resolve it and keep the chapter honest to its own standard.
3. **Source the Taleb, Legg-Hutter, Hampton, and calibration-baseline-range claims.** The turkey problem and Mediocristan/Extremistan are load-bearing and untagged; the "most students score 0.4–0.6 / 0.6–0.75 / 0.7–0.85" ranges are a strong empirical claim about learning outcomes with no data behind them — either cite the course data or reframe as expected-pattern-not-measured.
4. **Answer the strong-functionalist counterargument in the body, not only in exercise A6.** The chapter concedes multimodal grounding "might constitute something more" and then leaves the strongest objection to the "meaning limit will disappear" position for the student to rebut. A paragraph engaging it directly would close the gap in Priority #1 and pre-empt the obvious reviewer objection.

Already tabular and load-bearing; a figure version keyed to *where each limit was met earlier in the book* (Ch.2 Hume, Ch.5 access-boundary, Ch.8 shift) would carry the "you have met this before" synthesis claim the prose makes three separate times.

- **PQ** — Lines 274–276, the three-baseline calibration trajectory (0.4–0.6 → 0.6–0.75 → 0.7–0.85). A figure showing the expected improvement arc would carry the empirical claim the prose asserts, and would visibly flag it as an *expected* pattern the student checks their own data against.
- **MC** — Lines 220–226, the three-tier delegation map. A decision-figure (task → checkable-by-inspection? → delegate freely / with verification / not at all) would carry the operational instrument the chapter derives from the cognitive profile, making the boundary rule visible rather than tabular.

## One thing done well
The extended-mind reframe of AI as a "cognitive extremophile" is the chapter's — and one of the book's — best single moves: "The current AI profile... describes not a universal intelligence but a specific cognitive niche. It is a cognitive extremophile, extraordinarily adapted to a narrow environment: the environment of symbolic pattern-matching over text and structured data" (line 238). What makes it land is that it is not a put-down — the chapter has just spent pages establishing that AI is *superhuman* in that niche — so the metaphor does real analytic work: it explains simultaneously why AI is so impressive and why treating it as general is a category error. It reconnects to the book's opening Definition Problem (Legg-Hutter's "wide range of environments") and turns the whole book's skepticism into a precise claim about *niche width* rather than a vague warning about limits. It is the rare closing-chapter metaphor that clarifies rather than decorates.
