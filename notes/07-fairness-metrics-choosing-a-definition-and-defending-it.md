# Editor Notes — Chapter 7: Fairness Metrics: Choosing a Definition and Defending It
*Editor pass: bookmap + ISE + Eddy, calibrated to Computational Skepticism for AI. Diagnosis only — no rewrite.*

## Verdict
This chapter is the most complete and best-cited of the three: it builds the fairness-impossibility result from an honest "you sit down to write the requirement" cold-open, proves it two ways (the PPV identity and Chouldechova's Bayes relation), grounds it in the real COMPAS/ProPublica–Northpointe dispute, and then does the thing the whole book is about — converts a statistical result into a values choice and demands a *defended* deliverable. The coverage is genuinely broad (group, individual, causal, GE Index) and the citations are real (Kleinberg et al. 2016, Chouldechova, Dwork et al. 2012, Kusner et al. 2017). What it fails at is the same house test the others fail: it anchors to Pearl's ladder and to Botspeak, but never to the four skeptical moves, the five supervisory capacities (invoked only in an *exercise*), the solve–verify asymmetry, or the fluency trap — so it floats free of the Ch.1 spine even while executing a defensible-verification discipline. The single most important fix: name the defended-choice deliverable as the chapter's instance of the solve–verify asymmetry and the supervisory capacities, in the prose — the exercise already does the mapping; the chapter body must.

## Logical map
- **Core claim:** Demographic parity, equalized odds, and calibration parity cannot all hold simultaneously when base rates differ across groups (a theorem, not a tooling limit); therefore the choice of which to satisfy is an irreducible values decision that must be defended in writing, not resolved technically.
- **Supporting evidence:** The PPV identity $\text{PPV}=p\,\text{TPR}/(p\,\text{TPR}+(1-p)\text{FPR})$; Chouldechova's $v/(1-v)=[p/(1-p)](t/f)$; two numeric tables (calibration-satisfying vs equalized-odds-satisfying, same base rates 0.6/0.3); the COMPAS case with the ProPublica-vs-Northpointe "both correct" resolution; Dwork's $(D,d)$-Lipschitz individual fairness; Kusner's counterfactual fairness with the abduction/action/prediction procedure; the Generalized Entropy Index and its within/between-group decomposition; the three-family debiasing toolkit.
- **Logical method:** Proof-then-application — derive an impossibility, instantiate it in a public controversy, then argue that the residue is a values choice and specify the deliverable that discharges it (the six-step defended choice).
- **Methodological soundness:** **Good.** The theorem is correctly proved and attributed; COMPAS is handled with unusual fairness to both sides; the values-claim framing is exactly right for the book. Short of High because the spine anchors are absent from the body, the impossibility's scope condition (base rates differ *and* not perfect prediction) is stated but the second escape hatch is under-emphasized, and several numeric table values are presented without showing they satisfy the very constraints (calibration) the section claims.

## Logical gaps (epistemological)
- **The illustrative tables assert calibration/equalized-odds satisfaction without demonstrating it.** The "calibration-satisfying version" table gives TPR/FPR/PPV/base-rate rows but never shows the numbers actually *are* calibrated (that realized rates match assigned probabilities) — the reader is asked to trust the table embodies the property it names. Given the chapter's own thesis (don't trust plausible-looking numbers), the tables should be shown to satisfy their claimed constraint or be labeled schematic.
- **"On most real datasets they do differ" is the load-bearing empirical premise and is asserted.** The theorem's bite depends on base rates differing in practice; the chapter states this as fact ("on most real datasets they do differ") without evidence. It is almost certainly true, but it is the premise that turns a conditional theorem into a universal problem, and it is uncited.
- **The COMPAS base-rate claim smuggles the construct problem it later flags.** The chapter says base rates of *re-arrest* differed across groups and treats that as the theorem's input — then, forty lines later, correctly warns that "re-arrest is not the construct society cares about." The tension (using a contested proxy's base rates as if they were ground truth to demonstrate an impossibility) is real and unaddressed at the point of use; it deserves a forward-flag where COMPAS is introduced, not only in the closing limits section.
- **Counterfactual fairness's dependence on an unverifiable causal model is stated but its severity understated.** "You need a causal model... more knowledge than the data alone provides" is correct, but the chapter presents the abduction/action/prediction procedure with worked confidence that may leave students thinking it is runnable in practice; the identifiability caveat lands only in "What would change my mind."
- **Unresolved `[verify]` / unsourced load-bearing items:** No explicit `[verify]` tags in this chapter (cleaner than 5 and 6 on that axis). Unsourced load-bearing claims to flag: (1) "on most real datasets they do differ" — the universalizing premise. (2) The two numeric example tables — internally unverified against the calibration constraint. (3) The COMPAS FPR figure "~2× the FPR of white defendants" — a specific empirical claim attributed to ProPublica in-text but without a citation anchor. (4) "Two of the nine Botspeak pillars (treated in full in Appendix A)" — forward-reference to material whose existence the reader must take on trust.

## Framework anchoring
Like Ch.5 and unlike Ch.6, the chapter mostly floats free of the Ch.1 spine. **Explicitly anchored:** (a) **Pearl's causal ladder** — "in Pearl's terms, Rung 1 and low Rung 2," and the counterfactual section is built on the do-calculus intervention $\hat{Y}_{A\leftarrow a}$; (b) **Botspeak pillars** (Ethical Reasoning, Stochastic Reasoning) — but these are the *book's other framework*, not the calibration spine. The four skeptical moves are absent by name. The **five supervisory capacities** appear only in exercise 13 ("map each step... onto plausibility auditing, problem formulation, tool orchestration, interpretive judgment, executive integration") — a strong mapping that belongs in the body, not buried as homework. The **solve–verify asymmetry** is *enacted* ("the standard answer is a number... this is not enough... the deliverable is a defended choice") but never named. The **Pebble** callback (Case #6, upstream-provider bias) ties to the casebook thread but not to a Ch.1 move. Net: fewer than two *spine* elements anchored explicitly in the body — a finding under the house test.

## Pedagogical check
- **Phenomenon-first?** **Partial.** Opens with a constructive puzzle ("I want to give you a problem... you sit down to write the requirement") rather than an observed failure — engaging and better than a definition, but it is a hypothetical you-build-it scenario, not an observable in the world; COMPAS (the real phenomenon) arrives only after the theorem.
- **Quantitative before qualitative conclusions?** **Yes.** The PPV arithmetic and the two numeric tables precede "one of them has to give... which one gives is not a technical question."
- **Real failure case present, well-placed?** **Yes.** COMPAS is real, well-sourced (ProPublica/Northpointe), and correctly placed *after* the theorem so the reader sees the abstraction instantiated — the strongest real-case handling of the three chapters.
- **Honeymoon-period broken?** **Yes.** The three group metrics are taught as individually reasonable, then shown to be jointly impossible, then complicated further by individual/causal/GE layers — a sustained return-and-complicate arc.
- **Scale / level consciousness?** **Yes.** The GE decomposition explicitly bridges within-group (individual) and between-group (group) levels and ties the ratio to the remedy — an unusually clean level-bridge.
- **What the student can DO after it (Bloom Apply+)?** **Yes.** The six-step defended-choice structure + Glimmer 7.1 give a repeatable Evaluate/Create-level capability ("Produce a defended metric choice for a specified deployment").

## Priority fixes (ranked, highest impact first)
1. **Anchor the defended-choice deliverable to the spine, in the body.** — *What:* Name the six-step defense as the chapter's instance of the solve–verify asymmetry (generating a metric is cheap; defending the choice is the human verification) and cite the five supervisory capacities where the defense is introduced. *Why:* House test — the chapter enacts the spine but never labels it, and the capacity mapping is currently exiled to exercise 13. *How:* Two or three sentences in "The defense as deliverable"; pull the exercise-13 mapping forward as a sentence.
2. **Show the example tables satisfy their claimed constraints — or label them schematic.** — *What:* Demonstrate the "calibration-satisfying" table is actually calibrated, or mark it illustrative. *Why:* The chapter's thesis is distrust of plausible numbers; its own numbers should meet that bar. *How:* One added row or a caption label.
3. **Flag the COMPAS construct tension at point of use.** — *What:* Where re-arrest base rates are introduced as the theorem's input, forward-flag that re-arrest is a contested proxy (the point made later). *Why:* Otherwise the chapter uses a construct it will disown, without warning. *How:* One clause and a pointer to the closing limits section.
4. **Cite the universalizing premise.** — *What:* Support or soften "on most real datasets they do differ." *Why:* It is the premise that makes the theorem bite everywhere. *How:* A citation or a hedge ("commonly").

## High-assertion zones (figure candidates)
- Fig 7.1 — the impossibility triangle. **MC** — "You can satisfy any two. The third breaks. The triangle is the theorem." This is the chapter's central claim; the figure must actually encode the pairwise-satisfiable / jointly-impossible structure, not (per the D3 prompt appendix) a generic 5-bar chart — mismatch flagged.
- The two numeric tables (calibration-satisfying vs equalized-odds-satisfying). **PQ** — these carry the "same model, different threshold, one property breaks" assertion; a paired figure that visually shows PPV diverging while TPR/FPR equalize (and vice versa) would make the trade-off checkable rather than asserted.
- Fig 7.6 — GE decomposition stacked bar. **PQ** — "total unfairness = within + between" is a quantitative decomposition claim; the stacked bar must show a real decomposition with the ratio that drives the remedy, not a placeholder.

## One thing done well
The COMPAS resolution: *"Both claims were true. They were measuring different fairness properties... ProPublica and Northpointe were not having a factual disagreement that more data could settle. They were having a values disagreement about which definition of fairness should win."* This is the chapter's thesis landed on the field's most contested public case, and it is handled with rare even-handedness — neither side is made the villain, both are shown correct on their own metric, and the disagreement is relocated from the factual to the values register precisely where the impossibility theorem predicts it must be. It is the exact move the book exists to teach — a statistically valid output that is wrong about the question that matters — demonstrated on a case the reader has likely only seen argued as a scandal.
