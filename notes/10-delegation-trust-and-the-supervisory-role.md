# Editor Notes — Chapter 10: Delegation, Trust, and the Supervisory Role
*Editor pass: bookmap + ISE + Eddy, calibrated to Computational Skepticism for AI. Diagnosis only — no rewrite.*

## Verdict
This is the book's operational keystone, and it knows it ("the operational center of the book"). It converts the five supervisory capacities from personality traits into concrete pipeline jobs with named artifacts, and it lands its thesis — "a delegation is a *contract* with explicit boundaries" — through a two-teams opening that is the cleanest hook in the stretch and a Paper Summarizer walkthrough detailed enough that a reader could actually build the thing. What it fails at is proportion and epistemic self-application: the walkthrough consumes roughly half the chapter and tips from *illustration* into *product demo* for Gru (the author's own tool, with a live commercial URL repeated three times), and the chapter that most preaches "verify before you trust" presents its own central claims — "overtrust is the dominant failure mode," the Boondoggle Score's validity — with zero external evidence, purely on authorial assertion. The single most important fix: cut the Gru/product scaffolding down to the pedagogical core so the *concepts* (handoff testability, the five questions, trust calibration) stand free of the specific tool, and source or explicitly flag-as-opinion the "dominant failure mode" claim.

## Logical map
- **Core claim:** A delegation is not a partition of labor ("the AI does this part") but a *contract* whose load-bearing element is a *testable handoff condition* — one a non-author can read, observe the system, and verify — and a pipeline that lacks testable handoffs is unsupervised even when it appears to be working.
- **Supporting evidence:** The two-teams loan-scoring parable (adoption-committee outcome); the Paper Summarizer worked walkthrough (six steps, three human/three AI, full Boondoggle format); the five Boondoggle questions; the trust-calibration triad (under/over/calibrated); the AI Use Disclosure as supervisory log; Broadbent's vigilance research (AI Wayback Machine).
- **Logical method:** Contrastive parable (two near-identical pipelines differing only in delegation documentation) → operationalization (each abstract capacity → concrete artifact) → extended worked example proving the AI/human boundary "is not aesthetic."
- **Methodological soundness:** **Good.** The core argument (testability as the standard) is sound and the walkthrough genuinely demonstrates it. Held below High by unsourced empirical assertions ("overtrust is the dominant failure mode"), the unvalidated Boondoggle Score presented as established method, and the proportion problem that lets a proprietary tool crowd the concepts.

## Logical gaps (epistemological)
- **"Overtrust is the dominant failure mode" — asserted, hedged, and load-bearing.** The chapter says "In my reading, *overtrust is the dominant failure mode in current AI deployments*." The "in my reading" is honest, but the claim then does real work (it motivates the whole trust-calibration section and the Disclosure design) as if established. A book about not mistaking fluent assertion for evidence should either cite the human-automation literature (Parasuraman & Riley on misuse/disuse is the obvious anchor, and Broadbent is already in the room) or foreground it explicitly as a hypothesis, not a finding.
- **The Boondoggle Score is presented as validated method but is self-sourced.** The five questions are reasonable, but the chapter never establishes that this particular instrument sorts tasks *correctly* — that its buckets predict real delegation outcomes. It is the author's tool, taught as if its diagnostic validity were settled. The chapter's own "what would change my mind" section admits no tool yet automates testable delegation maps, but doesn't apply the same skepticism to whether the Boondoggle questions themselves are the right five.
- **Testability is claimed to be objective; the examples show it is graded.** "A non-author can read the condition, observe the system, and determine whether the condition is met." But the testable examples still embed judgment — "the most informative support available," "accurate in the domain-specific sense" (Step 4) — which are exactly the interpretive terms the chapter elsewhere says make a condition untestable. The chapter doesn't acknowledge that its own gold-standard human steps contain irreducibly interpretive handoff conditions, which complicates the clean testable/untestable binary.
- **The two-teams parable proves documentation quality, not outcome quality.** The committee tables Team 1 and can-evaluate Team 2 — but the chapter concedes "they may still reject" Team 2. So the parable establishes that good delegation maps survive *review*, not that they produce *better decisions*. The prose occasionally lets the reader slide from "passes adoption review" to "is a better pipeline," and those are different claims.
- **Unresolved `[verify]` / unsourced load-bearing claims:** No literal `[verify]` tags in this chapter (worth noting — it is the cleanest on that axis). But several unsourced load-bearing assertions function identically: "overtrust is the dominant failure mode"; the Broadbent biographical claims (APU 1958–1974, *Perception and Communication* 1958, *Decision and Stress* 1971) stated as fact in the Wayback section; and the implicit claim that the Boondoggle five-question set is a validated sorting instrument. The Broadbent portrait is explicitly AI-generated (disclosed — good), but the biographical facts around it are uncited.

## Framework anchoring
Explicitly anchored to **≥2** spine elements, densely. The **five supervisory capacities** are the chapter's organizing spine — each is operationalized into an artifact in a dedicated table and then instantiated as actual steps in the walkthrough. The **solve-verify asymmetry** is named explicitly and correctly restored to direction ("Chapter 1's solve-verify asymmetry, restored to its original direction") as the *foundation* of the verification-cost Boondoggle question. The **fluency trap** is named explicitly and located precisely — "This is the fluency trap operating at the workflow level: the AI's outputs will be fluent and specific, and the pull toward trusting them and moving on will be strong." That is three spine elements named, not merely invoked — the best anchoring in the stretch. The **four skeptical moves** are not named (no Cartesian/Humean/Popperian/Cave language), which is defensible for an operational chapter but means the chapter sits on the capacities/asymmetry/fluency half of the spine and never touches the epistemology half. Net: firmly anchored, does not float.

## Pedagogical check
- **Phenomenon-first?** **Yes.** Opens on two teams and a concrete outcome (one rollout tabled, one evaluated), not a definition of "delegation." The definition arrives only after the parable has made the reader want it.
- **Quantitative before qualitative conclusions?** **Partial.** The testable-condition examples are appropriately quantitative ("FPR equalized to within 2 pp," "sensitivity ≥ 0.95," override-rate exercise). But the headline qualitative claim ("overtrust is dominant") precedes and lacks any quantification.
- **Real failure case present, well-placed?** **Partial.** The two-teams parable is a constructed contrast, not a documented failure; the chapter leans on Ch.9's Ash case by reference rather than presenting a real Ch.10-specific failure. The "first team cannot answer" scenario is illustrative, not a case.
- **Honeymoon-period broken?** **Yes.** The Paper Summarizer starts as a simple happy-path flow and is progressively complicated — the "EI gap" flag at the end reveals the simple six-step version is incomplete for team settings, returning to and complicating the initial model.
- **Scale/level consciousness?** **Yes.** The "Where the map scales — and where it does not" section explicitly handles the ten-step → hundred-step → thousands-of-steps jump via hierarchical mapping, and honestly flags that the general multi-agent case is unsolved.
- **What the student can DO after?** **Yes (Bloom Apply/Create+).** Glimmer 10.1 (build a delegation map for your own project, lock predictions, peer-test the handoff conditions, revise) is a genuine Create-level capability, and the eight-item map structure is directly reusable.

## Priority fixes (ranked, highest impact first)
1. **Right-size the Gru/product content so the concepts stand alone.** What: the walkthrough and its three repetitions of the commercial URL consume ~half the chapter and bind the pedagogy to one proprietary tool. Why: a reader without Gru should still learn testable handoffs, the five questions, and trust calibration; right now the tool is load-bearing where the *method* should be. How: keep the walkthrough but demote Gru to "one tool that implements this" and ensure every concept is stated tool-independently first.
2. **Source or reframe "overtrust is the dominant failure mode."** What: an unsourced empirical claim doing structural work. Why: the book's own standard forbids fluent assertion as evidence, and this is the chapter that most preaches it. How: cite the automation-trust literature (Parasuraman/Riley, Lee & See on trust calibration) or state it explicitly as a working hypothesis.
3. **Acknowledge that the gold-standard human handoff conditions are themselves interpretive.** What: the clean testable/untestable binary vs. Step 4/6's "domain-specific sense," "would mislead a peer." Why: the chapter's central standard is more graded than it admits. How: one paragraph conceding that some handoff conditions are testable-in-principle only by a qualified reviewer, which is a narrower guarantee than "any non-author."
4. **Establish (or flag) the Boondoggle Score's validity.** What: the five-question instrument taught as settled method. Why: its diagnostic validity is asserted, not shown. How: either cite grounding for the five dimensions or note that the *questions* matter more than the *score* (the chapter half-says this — "The score is less important than the questions" — but then produces a numeric score anyway).
5. **Cite the Broadbent biographical facts.** What: dates and titles in the Wayback section stated as fact. Why: minor but it's the one place the chapter narrates history without provenance, and the section's whole point is verifying what a model got right.

## High-assertion zones (figure candidates)
- **Testable vs. untestable handoff condition.** VG — the chapter's central distinction lives entirely in prose and a table; a side-by-side showing five reviewers diverging on "reasonable/reviews" vs. converging on the specified version would carry the claim the prose asserts.
- **The three trust-calibration failure modes (Fig 10.4 exists).** PQ — under/over/calibrated is a claim about the *relationship* between reliance and actual reliability; the figure should plot reliance against true error rate with the three regimes marked, not just illustrate.
- **Boondoggle Score step timeline (Fig 10.3 exists).** VG — "three of six steps are irreducibly human" is the payoff assertion; the figure verifying which steps are human and why (the AI does pattern work, the human does judgment work) does load the prose can't.
- **The eight-item map, items 5–8 as the supervisory delta.** MC — the claim "most deployments have 1–4 and omit 5–8" is the chapter's structural diagnosis; a figure showing the two halves (engineering baseline vs. supervisory additions) would make the omission visible at a glance.

## One thing done well
The two-teams opening is a near-perfect instance of the book's own method turned into pedagogy. It holds everything constant — "Same data. Same model. Same fairness metrics. Same monitoring infrastructure" — so the *only* variable is the delegation map, and then it runs both through the identical adoption-committee test and shows the divergence. This is controlled-comparison reasoning dressed as narrative: the reader cannot attribute Team 1's failure to anything but the missing contract, because everything else was pinned. It also delivers the chapter's thesis experientially before stating it — by the time "a delegation is a *contract*" appears, the reader has already felt why, which is exactly the phenomenon-first discipline the book asks its chapters to model.
