# Editor Notes — Chapter 6: Model Explainability: Distinguishing Explanation from the Appearance of Explanation
*Editor pass: bookmap + ISE + Eddy, calibrated to Computational Skepticism for AI. Diagnosis only — no rewrite.*

## Verdict
This chapter does the hardest thing in the book — teach the Shapley mathematics rigorously *and* argue that the mathematics is epistemically insufficient — and it mostly succeeds: the radiologist cold-open, the "technically accurate, practically misleading" thesis, the transparency/explainability/interpretability trichotomy, and the Ash language-game analysis are all sharp and mutually reinforcing. It is the chapter that most explicitly anchors to the spine (Pearl's rungs are named on nearly every page; the Ash case ties to the calibration's Canonical failure 2). What it fails at is proportion and one silent inconsistency: the derivation section is long enough to become the honeymoon it never breaks — the reader can leave over-trusting SHAP's precision — and the axiom table renames "Additivity" to "Linearity" mid-chapter without comment, a lapse the book's own standards should catch. The single most important fix: reconnect the mathematics to the misleading-explanation thesis at the *end* of the math section, so the rigor serves the skepticism instead of competing with it — and reconcile the Additivity/Linearity naming.

## Logical map
- **Core claim:** Technically accurate explanations can be practically misleading, and this is more dangerous than no explanation, because a correct explanation of the model's internal accounting does epistemic work it cannot warrant — it describes the model, not the world, and the reader's language game differs from the model's.
- **Supporting evidence:** The radiologist/benign-biopsy case (explanation increased confidence in the wrong direction via a learned shortcut); full Shapley derivation with the four axioms and a numeric worked example (loan, $\phi$ summing to 0.17); SHAP/LIME/counterfactual comparison across Pearl rungs; the correlated-feature (marginal vs conditional) problem; the transparency/explainability/interpretability trichotomy; the Ash "the secret has been deleted" case as a language-game mismatch; Wittgenstein on language games.
- **Logical method:** Named-mechanism teaching (derive the guarantee, then show precisely what the guarantee does *not* cover) fused with a philosophical reframe (language games) that generalizes the limitation from feature-attribution to all model self-report.
- **Methodological soundness:** **Good.** The mathematics is correct and the axioms are stated precisely; the Pearl-rung anchoring is disciplined; the Ash analysis is the book's best worked instance of the spine. It misses High because of the Additivity→Linearity inconsistency, the unattributed radiologist case, and the length imbalance that risks leaving the student more impressed by SHAP than the thesis warrants.

## Logical gaps (epistemological)
- **Additivity vs Linearity naming inconsistency.** The prose defines the fourth axiom as **Additivity** ("If we have two games... the Shapley values for the combined game are the sums"), but the summary axiom table (after the worked example) lists the fourth axiom as **Linearity**. These are related but not identical properties in the cooperative-game literature; the chapter never signals the switch. A careful student will read it as an error or a hidden distinction. Load-bearing because the axioms are the chapter's guarantee.
- **The radiologist case is unsourced and load-bearing.** The opening — "The biopsy is benign" — carries the entire "practically misleading" thesis but is presented as a bare anecdote with no citation and no `[verify]` tag. Like Ch.5's opening, this is a fluent, plausible story doing evidentiary work the book's own standard says to distrust.
- **"The case where we most need the explanation is exactly the case where the model is misaligned" is asserted, not argued.** It is stated as an aphorism (and re-used in exercise S4), but the chapter never demonstrates that need and misalignment co-occur — it's intuitively appealing and possibly true, but it is a claim exceeding the evidence presented.
- **The Ash case is presented as fact without on-page attribution.** "Consider the agent we have been following — Ash's agent" and "the data persists on Proton's servers" are treated as established; the calibration ties this to Shapira et al., *Agents of Chaos*, Case #1, but the citation does not appear in this chapter (it points backward to "an earlier chapter"). A reader landing here cannot verify the case.
- **Unresolved `[verify]` / unsourced load-bearing items:** (1) Explicit `[verify and update.]` in the "What would change my mind" section (causal feature-importance work). (2) The radiologist/benign-biopsy case — unsourced, load-bearing. (3) The Ash/Proton deletion case — factual claims ("persists on Proton's servers," backups, recovery window) with no on-page source. (4) "The shap package uses 10 permutations by default" and TreeSHAP complexity $O(TLD)$ — specific technical claims stated without citation; the default especially will age.

## Framework anchoring
This is the best-anchored of the three chapters. **Explicitly anchored (≥2 met):** (a) **Pearl's ladder / Rung 1 vs Rung 2** is named continuously and is the load-bearing frame for the SHAP-is-associative argument — "SHAP operates entirely at Pearl's Rung 1"; (b) the **Ash case** explicitly instantiates the calibration's Canonical failure 2, and the chapter names it as such ("the agent did not lie"). Also present: the **fluency trap** — "its fluency makes the user MORE confident in the wrong direction" — though named only in the downstream LLM exercise, not the body; and the **five supervisory capacities** appear only glancingly ("the supervisory move, then, is a question"). Where it floats: the four skeptical moves of Ch.1 are not invoked by name (the language-game critique is doing Plato's-Cave-adjacent work — model's world vs real world — but is anchored to Wittgenstein instead of the spine). Recommendation stands to name the fluency trap in the body where the radiologist grows "more confident in the wrong direction" — that is the trap's definition.

## Pedagogical check
- **Phenomenon-first?** **Yes.** "A radiologist looks at a screening image... The biopsy is benign" — an observable failure before any definition of SHAP.
- **Quantitative before qualitative conclusions?** **Yes.** The worked Shapley example ($\phi_{x_1}=0.073$, Efficiency check to 0.17) precedes the qualitative claim that the zip-code arrow "is real. It is not causal."
- **Real failure case present, well-placed?** **Partial.** Two strong cases (radiologist, Ash) bookend the chapter — well-placed — but both are unsourced on the page.
- **Honeymoon-period broken?** **Partial.** The chapter *does* return to complicate SHAP (correlated features, marginal vs conditional), but the derivation is long enough that the complication arrives late; a student could finish the math section over-trusting the method before the caveats land. The break happens, but the timing lets the honeymoon run.
- **Scale / level consciousness?** **Yes.** The Ash "deletion at one level... persistence at another" and "Rung 2 in the model is not Rung 2 in the world" are explicit level-bridging.
- **What the student can DO after it (Bloom Apply+)?** **Yes.** "Use the 'audience question' as a supervisory check" plus Glimmer 6.1 give a concrete Analyze/Evaluate-level capability applied to a real tool.

## Priority fixes (ranked, highest impact first)
1. **Reconcile Additivity vs Linearity.** — *What:* Use one name, or explicitly note they are the same property under a name change. *Why:* The axioms are the chapter's formal guarantee; an unexplained rename reads as an error and undermines the rigor the section is built on. *How:* One-line edit in the table or a parenthetical ("Additivity, sometimes called Linearity").
2. **Break the honeymoon earlier / rebalance the math.** — *What:* Move a one-sentence forward-reference to the "explains the model, not the world" limit into or right after the axiom section, before the reader banks the precision. *Why:* The derivation's length currently lets SHAP feel more trustworthy than the thesis allows — the chapter risks producing the exact over-confidence it warns about. *How:* A single bridging sentence; no cutting required.
3. **Source or tag the radiologist case.** — *What:* Add a `[verify]` tag or label it a composite. *Why:* Same dog-food violation as Ch.5 — a fluent anecdote carries the thesis. *How:* Footnote or inline label.
4. **Name the fluency trap in the body.** — *What:* At "made the radiologist more confident in the wrong direction," name it as the fluency trap. *Why:* Explicit spine anchoring; the passage is already a textbook instance. *How:* Two words.

## One thing done well
The transparency/explainability/interpretability trichotomy and its punchline: *"A system that meets a SHAP-attribution requirement on every prediction has not, by that fact, become interpretable to the loan applicant or the patient or the defendant... Somebody has signed off on the wrong property."* This is the chapter's most portable contribution — it takes three words the literature uses interchangeably, separates them with counterexamples (transparent-but-uninterpretable 175B-parameter model; explainable-but-uninterpretable attributions), and lands the separation on a concrete institutional failure (procurement language demanding one property and meaning another). It converts a philosophical distinction into an audit question a student can carry into a real contract review: *explainable to whom?*
