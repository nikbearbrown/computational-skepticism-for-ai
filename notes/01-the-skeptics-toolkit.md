# Editor Notes — Chapter 01: The Skeptic's Toolkit
*Editor pass: bookmap + ISE + Eddy, calibrated to Computational Skepticism for AI. Diagnosis only — no rewrite.*

## Verdict
This is the strongest possible opening the book could have: it installs the entire spine — four moves, five capacities, solve–verify asymmetry, fluency trap — in one chapter, and it does so with a phenomenon-first structure (two failures before any definition) that models the discipline it teaches. The prose is disciplined, the repetition is deliberate rather than lazy ("the model's confidence is a property of the model, not a property of the world"), and it eats its own dog food by naming what would change its mind. What it fails at is provenance: its two load-bearing anchor cases — the Swedish triage death and Ash's agent — are *both* flagged `[verify]`, and the entire book leans on them. The single most important fix is to resolve the Swedish case to a primary source (or substitute one), because a book about not trusting fluent-but-unverified claims cannot open on a composite case it has not verified — that is the Fluency Trap turned inward, and a reviewer will notice.

## Logical map
- **Core claim:** An AI output can be statistically valid by every internal metric and still be wrong about the question that matters; catching that gap requires human supervisory capacities that metrics cannot see, deployed as a teachable method (not a disposition).
- **Supporting evidence:** The Swedish triage case (`[verify]` composite); the Ash/*Agents of Chaos* Case #1 agent failure (`[verify]`); Taleb's turkey; Descartes/Hume/Popper as named donors of moves; the rhetorical-vs-Popperian claim table; the five-capacity table with failure modes and forward chapter references.
- **Logical method:** Argument by structural analogy across two disparate failures ("Both failures share a structure"), then decomposition into a named toolkit (four moves) and a taxonomy (five capacities). It generalizes from two cases to a universal claim about AI deployment.
- **Methodological soundness:** **Good.** The reasoning is coherent and self-aware, and the chapter explicitly limits its own claims ("What would change my mind"). It is held back from High only by the two unverified anchor cases carrying the full inductive weight of a universal claim.

## Logical gaps (epistemological)
- **Two cases → universal claim.** "Both failures share a structure that runs through this entire book" generalizes from n=2 to every AI deployment. The chapter does not acknowledge that two cases, however vivid, are a thin inductive base for a book-spanning thesis — a mild irony given the chapter itself teaches Hume's limit on induction. A third structurally different case, or an explicit note that the generalization is a conjecture to be tested against later chapters, would close this.
- **"Verification cannot be automated" asserted, then softened.** The claim "The answer is *never* 'automate the verification'" (§solve-verify) is absolute, but Exercise 7 asks the student to construct the counterargument — so the chapter knows the claim is contestable yet states it without hedge in the body. The load-bearing word "never" exceeds the evidence given.
- **The fluency-trap second stage is asserted, not evidenced.** "the more confident the user becomes in the output, the more confident the user becomes *in their own evaluation of the output*" is a specific psychological claim presented as mechanism with no citation or study. It is plausible and central; it currently rests on rhetoric.
- **Unresolved `[verify]` / unsourced load-bearing claims:**
  - Line 13 — Swedish triage case: `[verify]` composite, "confirm against Lindgren et al. or substitute a primary-sourced case before publication." **This is the book's Canonical Failure 1 and must be resolved.**
  - Line 17 — Ash / *Agents of Chaos: Eleven Red-Team Studies of Agentic Failure*, 2026, Case #1: `[verify]`. This is the book's Pebble/longitudinal case and Canonical Failure 2; the source is a forward-dated (2026) citation that needs to resolve to something real or be re-labeled as constructed.
  - The fluency-trap two-stage mechanism (line 172) — no source.

## Framework anchoring
This chapter *is* the spine, so anchoring is definitional rather than tested — and all four elements are explicit: the four skeptical moves (Descartes/Hume/Popper/Plato's Cave, §What I mean by skepticism + §The cave), the five supervisory capacities (§The Five Supervisory Capacities, with table), the solve–verify asymmetry (§The solve-verify asymmetry), and the fluency trap (§The fluency trap). Far more than the required ≥2. Nothing floats free. The only anchoring risk is forward-looking: the chapter promises each capacity maps to a later chapter (table, col 4) — those promises are now load-bearing constraints on Chapters 3, 4, 6, 8, 13.

## Pedagogical check
- **Phenomenon-first?** **Yes.** Opens on the woman in the Swedish ED, not a definition — "In 2018, a 49-year-old woman walked into an emergency department." Definition of skepticism is deferred to line 44.
- **Quantitative before qualitative conclusions?** **Partial.** By design this is the conceptual chapter ("do not depend on any mathematics — those come in Chapter 2"), so it is appropriately qualitative. The Popper table gives concrete thresholds (error rate <2%, precision ≥0.91) which grounds the abstraction.
- **Real failure case present, well-placed?** **Yes** — two of them, front-loaded, and one (Ash) explicitly seeded as recurring. Placement is exemplary; sourcing is the problem (see gaps).
- **Honeymoon-period broken?** **Yes.** "the Swedish system was not broken... The system worked exactly as designed" deliberately complicates the reader's first assumption that failure = malfunction.
- **Scale / level consciousness?** **Yes.** The §solve-verify asymmetry section explicitly moves between the per-output level (fractions of a cent) and the system level (verification does not happen at scale), no unbridged jump.
- **"What the student can DO" after it?** **Yes.** Explicit "What you will be able to do" list (Apply the four moves, Name/distinguish five capacities, Identify the fluency trap, Map a failure to a capacity) — all Bloom Apply+ verbs.

## Priority fixes (ranked, highest impact first)
1. **Resolve the Swedish triage case to a primary source, or substitute one.** — What: replace the `[verify]` composite with a documented, citable deployment. Why: it is Canonical Failure 1, the book's opening image, and an unverified composite in a skepticism book is a self-inflicted wound. How: source a real triage-AI adverse event, or relabel explicitly as a constructed composite and lean on Ash as the primary real anchor.
2. **Resolve or re-frame the *Agents of Chaos* / Ash citation.** — What: confirm the 2026 Shapira et al. paper exists and Case #1 says what is claimed, or mark Ash as a constructed teaching case. Why: it recurs in six later chapters; if it dissolves, so do they. How: verify the citation now, before Chapters 5–13 are drafted against it.
3. **Hedge or evidence the two absolute claims.** — What: "verification can *never* be automated" and the fluency-trap second stage. Why: the body states as fact what the exercises treat as contestable. How: add one clause acknowledging the counterargument the chapter itself later solicits (Ex. 7), and cite or soften the "confidence-in-evaluation" mechanism.

## High-assertion zones (figure candidates)
- **Fluency trap two-stage mechanism** (§The fluency trap, fig 1.6) — MC: fluency → confidence-in-output → confidence-in-own-evaluation is a ≥3-step causal claim the prose asserts; a mechanism diagram would make the second stage inspectable rather than merely stated.
- **Solve–verify asymmetry inversion** (§solve-verify, fig 1.5) — PQ: the whole point is a proportional claim (production ≈ fractions of a cent vs verification ≈ an hour of senior labor). The current fig brief is a generic "bar chart"; it should carry the actual cost ratio.
- **Four moves as inspection protocol** (figs 1.1, 1.3) — MC: Cartesian doubt "produces a checklist" — the move-to-checklist transformation is a multi-step assertion the prose makes and a figure could verify. *Note for production: every figure prompt in the §Prompts appendix is a boilerplate "horizontal bar chart with 5 labeled categories," which does not match any of these assertions — the figure specs are placeholders and contradict the captions.*

## One thing done well
The refusal to let the reader off with "the system was buggy." "Now, the Swedish system was not broken. I want you to sit with that for a second, because it is the whole point... By every measure the engineers had built into the system, the system had succeeded. A patient was dead. The system had succeeded." This is the honeymoon-breaking move executed perfectly: it takes the reader's most natural inference (failure implies malfunction) and dismantles it in four sentences, and the flat, repeated "The system had succeeded" does the emotional and logical work simultaneously. Everything the book needs the reader to believe is compressed into that beat.
