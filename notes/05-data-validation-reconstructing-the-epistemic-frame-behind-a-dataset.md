# Editor Notes — Chapter 5: Data Validation: Reconstructing the Epistemic Frame Behind a Dataset
*Editor pass: bookmap + ISE + Eddy, calibrated to Computational Skepticism for AI. Diagnosis only — no rewrite.*

## Verdict
This is the strongest chapter of the three and one of the best-constructed in the book: it opens with a genuine, well-told failure (the four-percent silent-join drop), extracts from it a single portable question — *"Why are there exactly N rows in this dataset?"* — and then earns its central distinction ("procedural EDA as evidence of competence, interrogation as evidence of understanding"). The pedagogy is disciplined: procedural pass first, marks-and-channels theory second, interrogation third, with a delegation table that operationalizes the skeptical stance. What it fails at is framework anchoring: the chapter is soaked in the book's *spirit* but never names the four skeptical moves, the five supervisory capacities, or the solve–verify asymmetry explicitly — the spine is present as mood, not as scaffolding, so a student cannot map this chapter back onto Chapter 1. The single most important fix: anchor the "clean dataset that destroyed a deployment" story to the Plato's Cave move and the interrogation-vs-procedure split to the solve–verify asymmetry, by name, in the prose — not in an editor's imagination.

## Logical map
- **Core claim:** Procedural EDA is necessary but structurally insufficient for deployment-ready validation, because it can only see what was written into the dataset; the load-bearing work is interrogating the epistemic frame — what the recording instrument could and could not see — which requires human engagement EDA cannot supply.
- **Supporting evidence:** The opening join-failure case (4% drop rate concentrated in one subpopulation via inconsistent identifier formatting); the six structural-assumption table (sampling, time-window, label, MNAR, feature-engineering, access/boundary); the autonomous email-agent access-boundary story; the MCAR/MAR/MNAR taxonomy; Cleveland & McGill / Stevens on channel effectiveness; the six-step reconstruction procedure with prediction-lock.
- **Logical method:** Argument from a diagnostic blind spot — demonstrate a failure invisible to the standard procedure, generalize the blind spot into a taxonomy, then supply a procedure that targets it. Reinforced by an epistemic reframe ("a dataset is an artifact, not a recording").
- **Methodological soundness:** **Good.** The core argument is sound and vividly evidenced; the delegation taxonomy and the "what the procedural pass cannot see" section are rigorous. It falls short of High because the opening case is unsourced/composite (no `[verify]` tag, but load-bearing), the two canonical framework anchors are absent, and several perceptual-science claims (Stevens, Cleveland & McGill) are asserted without citation.

## Logical gaps (epistemological)
- **The opening case is load-bearing and unsourced.** The entire chapter rests on "a dataset that destroyed a deployment" — yet it carries no `[verify]` tag and no citation, presented as a remembered war story. It is plausible and instructive, but by the book's own dog-food standard (a fluent story read as evidence) it should be flagged as composite/illustrative or sourced. This is the chapter's biggest self-consistency risk.
- **Stevens' power law and Cleveland & McGill are asserted, not cited.** "Stevens' psychophysical power law gives us a framework" and "based on Cleveland & McGill's landmark perceptual studies" (Fig 5.7 caption) are load-bearing empirical claims presented without reference. A reader cannot check the effectiveness hierarchy; it is taken on the author's authority — exactly the fluency the book warns against.
- **The "trace one row end to end" step assumes access that often doesn't exist.** Step 5 ("Follow its values back to the source systems") is presented as universally doable, but for inherited datasets, third-party corpora, or foundation-model training data the source systems are unreachable — a limitation the chapter acknowledges elsewhere (the LLM exercise notes "usually opaque") but not at the point of instruction. The step overreaches its stated generality.
- **The access-boundary claim slides from "email" to "almost all data" without argument.** "Email messages, like almost all naturally occurring data, are not bounded by their schema" — the leap from one vivid case to a near-universal claim is asserted, not defended. A structured tabular dataset of temperature readings does not obviously "reference the world outside itself" in the same way; the counter-case is not addressed.
- **Unresolved `[verify]` / unsourced load-bearing items:** (1) The "What would change my mind" section carries an explicit `[Verify: current scope of Great Expectations, Deequ, and successor tools before publication.]` (2) The opening join-failure case — unsourced, load-bearing, no tag. (3) Stevens' power law — uncited. (4) Cleveland & McGill "landmark perceptual studies" — uncited. (5) The email-agent access-boundary story — presented as fact ("I want to tell you a story about an agent"); it echoes the calibration's Canonical failure 2 (Shapira et al., Agents of Chaos) but is not attributed here.

## Framework anchoring
The chapter invokes the spine repeatedly but almost never by name — this is the central finding. Present *implicitly*: (a) the **Plato's Cave move** — "a dataset is not a faithful recording of the world; it is an artifact" and "clean of the things histograms can see" is the shadows-on-the-wall argument, unnamed; (b) the **solve–verify asymmetry** — "the procedural work is evidence of competence; the interrogation is evidence of understanding" is precisely generate-cheap / verify-hard, unnamed; (c) **Humean induction limits** — the sampling and time-window assumptions ("you train on convenience samples and deploy against the world") are induction-limit arguments, unnamed. **Fewer than two are anchored explicitly** — the chapter floats free of the spine's vocabulary throughout, even while executing its logic faithfully. The five supervisory capacities appear only in the downstream LLM exercise, not the chapter body. This is a *finding*, not a nit, per the house test.

## Pedagogical check
- **Phenomenon-first?** **Yes.** Opens with an observable failure ("I want to tell you about a dataset that destroyed a deployment"), not a definition of EDA.
- **Quantitative before qualitative conclusions?** **Yes.** The "four-percent drop rate" and the concrete `.describe()`/histogram mechanics precede the "artifact not recording" conclusion.
- **Real failure case present, well-placed?** **Partial.** The join-failure case is excellent and front-loaded, but it is unsourced/composite; the email-agent case is real-in-spirit but unattributed. Well-placed, not fully grounded.
- **Honeymoon-period broken?** **Yes.** The procedural pass is taught straight and fully ("Run the procedure first"), then explicitly complicated ("the procedure is not the *work*") — a clean simple-model-returned-to-and-complicated arc.
- **Scale / level consciousness?** **Yes.** The access-boundary section is explicitly about levels ("deletion at one level of the system and persistence at another" is the sibling case in Ch.6; here: "the boundary is the schema plus everything its contents touch") — no unbridged jumps.
- **What the student can DO after it (Bloom Apply+)?** **Yes.** The six-step procedure + prediction-lock + Glimmer 5.1 give a concrete, repeatable capability at Apply/Analyze level ("Trace a dataset failure backward to the structural assumption that produced it").

## Priority fixes (ranked, highest impact first)
1. **Anchor to the spine explicitly.** — *What:* Name the Plato's Cave move where "a dataset is an artifact" lands, and name the solve–verify asymmetry where "competence vs understanding" lands. *Why:* The house test requires ≥2 explicit anchors; right now the chapter executes the framework without ever letting the student map it back to Ch.1. *How:* Two or three sentences at existing junctures — no restructuring needed; the arguments are already there, just unlabeled.
2. **Source or tag the opening case.** — *What:* Add a `[verify]` tag or an explicit "composite illustration" label to the join-failure story. *Why:* The chapter's own thesis is that fluent, plausible narratives shouldn't be trusted as evidence; an unsourced war story carrying the whole chapter violates that inward. *How:* One footnote or an inline "(a composite of failures I've seen)".
3. **Cite the perceptual-science claims.** — *What:* Reference Cleveland & McGill (1984) and Stevens' power law. *Why:* Load-bearing empirical claims about channel effectiveness are currently on authority alone. *How:* Inline citations; the Fig 5.7 caption already names Cleveland & McGill — promote it into the prose.
4. **Bound the "trace one row" and "almost all data" claims.** — *What:* Add the access caveat at Step 5 and defend or narrow the "almost all naturally occurring data" leap. *Why:* Both overreach their evidence. *How:* A clause each ("where you have source-system access") and one sentence distinguishing reference-rich from self-contained data.

## One thing done well
The delegation table (§Strategic delegation) and its caption: *"The line is not about AI capability — it is about what the work produces. Mechanical outputs have determinate right answers the AI can reach. The interrogation produces understanding that only comes from you confronting the data yourself."* This is the chapter's thesis rendered operational — it converts an abstract epistemic distinction (competence vs understanding) into a three-way triage a student can actually apply to a real workflow, and it correctly locates the reason for the line in *what the output is evidence of* rather than in AI limitations. It is the solve–verify asymmetry made into a tool, and it is the single most transferable page in the chapter.
