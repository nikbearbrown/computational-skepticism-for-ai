# Editor Notes — Chapter 02: Probability, Uncertainty, and the Confidence Illusion
*Editor pass: bookmap + ISE + Eddy, calibrated to Computational Skepticism for AI. Diagnosis only — no rewrite.*

## Verdict
This is a genuinely good probability chapter that earns its keep by refusing to teach probability for its own sake: every tool (conditional probability, chain rule, total probability, Bayes, calibration, heavy tails) is bent toward the single question of whether a model's stated number means what the engineer thinks it means, and the base-rate section does the math *slowly, on the page*, exactly as promised. It is quantitative-before-qualitative done right. What it fails at is framework anchoring relative to the book's spine: the chapter leans hard on Hume (one of the four moves) but lets Descartes, Popper, and the Plato's Cave move go unnamed, and it barely touches the five supervisory capacities or the fluency trap by name — so it reads slightly like an excellent standalone stats chapter bolted to the book rather than growing from it. The single most important fix is to explicitly thread ≥2 more spine elements (Popperian falsifiability of a calibration claim; interpretive-judgment capacity on the "0.87" reading) so the chapter is anchored, not adjacent.

## Logical map
- **Core claim:** A model's stated probability is not a fact about the world but a claim conditional on base rate, calibration, loss distribution, and distributional stability; ignore any of the four and the confident number becomes an illusion — and intuition reliably ignores the first (the prior).
- **Supporting evidence:** The three axioms; the 99%-accurate/1-in-10,000 disease worked example (the load-bearing set piece); the manufacturing-defect and Alice-Markov worked examples; the calibration-curve construction; the CLT-failure/heavy-tail argument; the pandemic medical-imaging distribution-shift case (`[verify]`); the softmax-overconfidence claim (`[verify]`).
- **Logical method:** Derivation from axioms (everything descends from the three rules), then a single counterintuitive worked calculation used as an intuition-pump, then structural generalization ("The false-positive structure is everywhere") across a domain table. Reduction of a philosophical problem (Hume) to an engineering one (distribution shift).
- **Methodological soundness:** **Good.** The math is correct and the pedagogical sequencing is disciplined. Held below High by three unresolved `[verify]` citations on the chapter's most empirically load-bearing claims and by the anchoring thinness against the book's own spine.

## Logical gaps (epistemological)
- **The "two engineers, different priors, both correct" claim smuggles in Bayesian subjectivism as settled.** Line 43: "Two engineers can look at exactly the same evidence, end up at different probabilities, and both be reasoning correctly." This is a contestable interpretive stance (a frequentist would object) presented as fact. The chapter later admits Bayesian priors are a "key limitation" (table, line 97) but the body states the subjectivist reading without flagging it as one position among several.
- **Base-rate example uses a colloquial "99% accurate" that conflates sensitivity and specificity.** The worked example (lines 261–265) treats "99% accurate" as simultaneously the true-positive rate and the true-negative rate. This is the standard textbook simplification, but a chapter whose whole thesis is *precision about what a number means* should name that "accuracy" is doing double duty here, or a sharp student will catch the book doing the loose thing it warns against.
- **Heavy-tail claim about the triage case is asserted retroactively without data.** Line 447: "That system was operating in a heavy-tailed loss world... The average loss across deployments was tiny." This is presented as established fact about the Chapter 1 case, but the Chapter 1 case is itself a `[verify]` composite — so a quantitative claim (average loss tiny, catastrophic loss the only one that mattered) is being layered onto an unverified case. The inference is plausible but the evidentiary base is fictional.
- **Unresolved `[verify]` / unsourced load-bearing claims:**
  - Line 386 — pandemic medical-imaging distribution shift: `[Verify: DeGrave et al. 2021, Nature Machine Intelligence]`. This is the chapter's *only* real-world distribution-shift case; if it dissolves, the Hume→distribution-shift section loses its empirical anchor.
  - Line 407 — softmax/loss-function overconfidence in modern nets: `[Verify: Guo et al. 2017, On Calibration of Modern Neural Networks]`. Load-bearing for the calibration section's central empirical claim.
  - Line 498 — out-of-distribution detection limits: `[Verify: Krell et al. 2024]`. Load-bearing for the "What would change my mind" falsification condition.

## Framework anchoring
**Under-anchored for the house test.** Explicitly invoked: **Hume's induction limit** (one of the four moves) — done thoroughly and well, §"Hume's induction problem, re-translated as distribution shift," and reprised as a refrain ("Hume on the page"). That is one spine element, richly developed. But: **Popper** is used implicitly (the four diagnostic questions are falsification conditions; "What would have to remain true...") yet never *named* as Popper's move, a missed free anchor. The **five supervisory capacities** appear only obliquely (the "0.87 means something else — or nothing" passage is pure interpretive-judgment but is not labeled as such). The **fluency trap** — arguably the perfect frame for "the number on the screen is decorative" — is never named, though it is exactly the phenomenon. The **solve–verify asymmetry** is absent. So the chapter clears the ≥2-explicit bar only if you credit Hume plus the implicit Popperian four-questions; that is thin. It floats free of the capacities and the fluency trap where it most naturally connects.

## Pedagogical check
- **Phenomenon-first?** **Partial.** The chapter proper opens with "Most of you came up through a Boolean education" — a claim about the reader, not an observable phenomenon. The real phenomenon-first hook (the 99% test that kills) arrives strong but late (line 247). The axioms come before the puzzle that motivates them.
- **Quantitative before qualitative conclusions?** **Yes** — this is the chapter's signature strength. The base-rate conclusion ("about one percent") is *derived* by counting bodies (1 true positive, ~100 false positives) before any moral is drawn, then re-derived formally via Bayes. Exactly the target ordering.
- **Real failure case present, well-placed?** **Partial.** The pandemic-imaging case (line 386) is real-if-verified and well-placed, but it is one sentence and `[verify]`. The chapter otherwise runs on constructed textbook examples (dice, cards, factories, Alice) — pedagogically fine, but the *deployment* failure evidence is thin for a book whose spine is real failures.
- **Honeymoon-period broken?** **Yes.** The CLT section is the return-and-complicate move: it hands the reader the comforting bell curve, then shows "the theorem has two requirements buried in it... When either requirement fails, the theorem fails, and the failure is usually quiet."
- **Scale / level consciousness?** **Yes.** Explicit movement between the single case (this patient) and the population (10,000 people taking the test), and between per-decision loss and deployment-average loss in the heavy-tail section. No unbridged jumps.
- **"What the student can DO" after it?** **Yes.** Learning objectives are Apply-level ("Apply Bayes' theorem to calculate...", "Identify the base rate...", "Recognize the conditions under which the CLT does not apply"), and the four-questions synthesis is a directly portable capability.

## High-assertion zones (figure candidates)
- **The base-rate population grid** (fig 2.5, "10,000 dots") — PQ: the entire argument is a proportional claim (1 sick vs ~100 false-positives out of 101 flagged). A true population grid carries the assertion the prose makes by counting; this is the chapter's most important figure and must show the *ratio*, not a generic bar chart.
- **Calibration curve** (fig 2.8) — VG/PQ: the shape of the peel-off from the diagonal *is* the diagnostic ("the *shape* of how it peels off tells you something"). A structure/topology figure showing well-calibrated middle vs overconfident high-end tail carries a claim the prose can only describe.
- **Heavy-tail vs Gaussian** (fig 2.9) — PQ: "the next observation can move your average dramatically no matter how many you have collected" is a quantitative claim about tail mass that a two-distribution overlay can verify visually. *Production note: as in Ch.1, every §Prompts figure spec is the same boilerplate "horizontal bar chart with 5 labeled categories," contradicting these captions — the specs are placeholders.*

## One thing done well
The bodies-not-formula derivation of the base rate. "Imagine ten thousand people taking the test. One of them, on average, actually has the disease... The other 1% — that is 0.01 times 9,999, about 100 people — get a false positive. So we have: one true positive, and about one hundred false positives... the chance it came from someone who is actually sick is 1/101, about one percent." This is the whole pedagogy of the book in miniature: the counterintuitive result is made *inevitable* by counting concrete people before any formula appears, so that when Bayes' theorem arrives it merely names what the reader has already been forced to see. The prediction-lock instruction beforehand ("Stop. Don't read on yet. Pick a number") converts the reader from spectator to subject, which is the frictional move the book will formalize in Chapter 4.
